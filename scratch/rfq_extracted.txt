1. Project Overview
Aurora Imaging Systems, Inc. (“Aurora”) is developing DermaPod©, a 360° full-body dermatological imaging system intended to support clinical screening, longitudinal lesion monitoring, and AI-assisted classification across a defined target set of dermatological conditions. The platform is being built for use in dermatology clinics, primary-care screening environments, and research settings.
This RFQ solicits proposals from qualified vendors to design, build, integrate, and deliver the camera and robotic control subsystem of DermaPod©. The subsystem must achieve full-body coverage of an adult patient (Fitzpatrick skin types I–VI), coordinated by a positioning and synchronization architecture that completes a full acquisition within a clinically acceptable Patient Cycle Time.
This RFQ is intentionally framed around performance, operational, clinical-workflow, and cost requirements rather than prescribed implementation approaches. Aurora’s prior design work, prototyping observations, and current architectural assumptions — including camera-count and robotic-architecture assumptions — are provided as informational reference material in Exhibit A — Design Experience and Current Assumptions. Exhibit A items are not mandatory requirements; vendors are encouraged to leverage them where useful and to propose superior or alternative approaches with supporting justification.
1.1 Clinical Performance Context
The imaging subsystem is expected to produce image quality sufficient to support Aurora’s intended clinical targets: classification of, at minimum, melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, psoriasis, seborrheic keratosis, and vitiligo, with Aurora targeting ≥0.85 classification accuracy on internal validation sets at the integrated-system level.
Aurora recognizes that final clinical accuracy outcomes depend on downstream factors outside the vendor’s scope — including AI interpretation, training datasets, model development, and validation methodologies. The vendor’s responsibility under this RFQ is therefore defined in terms of measurable image-quality, coverage, color-fidelity, and acquisition-performance requirements (Sections 4–6), not end-to-end diagnostic accuracy.
Image quality must support both pigmented-lesion analysis (cross-polarized capture) and surface/textural analysis (non-polarized capture).
1.2 Out of Scope for This RFQ
The following are explicitly outside the scope of this procurement and will be developed or sourced separately by Aurora:
Downstream AI classification models and inference pipeline.
Patient-facing clinical user interface and EMR integration.
Cloud storage, PHI handling, and identity management.
Cabinet and enclosure industrial design (the vendor must accommodate the volumetric and ingress envelope specified in Section 6).
2. Procurement Process, Phasing, and Engagement Model
2.1 Two-Phase Development Approach
Phase 1 — Prototype / Proof-of-Concept (this RFQ). This RFQ covers the design, build, integration, and delivery of one (1) POC/prototype unit only, together with the Phase 1 deliverables in Section 10.
Phase 2 — Commercial-Ready Systems (future). Aurora’s current intention is to manufacture approximately ten (10) systems for future clinical trials, followed by commercial scale-up. Phase 2 quantities, milestone structures, progress payments, commercial production terms, and broader manufacturing considerations will be developed during Phase 2 / final contracting discussions and are not committed under this RFQ. Phase 2 pricing requested in Section 11 is indicative and used for total-cost-of-ownership evaluation.
Deliverable, documentation, and regulatory expectations in this RFQ are scoped to the prototype stage unless explicitly noted otherwise. Compliance-ready and production/commercialization expectations will be defined and contracted in Phase 2.
2.2 Engagement Philosophy
Aurora intends this procurement to support open technical dialogue, early risk identification, and collaborative engineering. Aurora values vendors who ask thoughtful technical questions, identify risks proactively, discuss tradeoffs honestly, recommend phased de-risking approaches, and challenge assumptions constructively. Vendor collaboration and communication quality are explicitly scored (Section 14).
Following proposal receipt, Aurora may conduct technical Q&A sessions, architecture discussions, clarification workshops, and vendor capability presentations prior to down-selection.
2.3 Multi-Vendor and Re-Scoping Rights
Aurora prefers a single integrated vendor for this subsystem but is prepared for a multi-vendor outcome. Aurora reserves the right to split portions of this RFQ across multiple vendors, to separately source subsystems or components, and to re-scope portions of the work if required. Vendors with strong capability in a subset of the scope are encouraged to respond and to clearly identify the scope they propose to deliver, including any assumed partners or interfaces.
2.4 Confidentiality and Disclosure Sequence
A mutual NDA shall be fully executed prior to distribution of complete RFQ materials and detailed technical disclosures, including Exhibit A. Substantive technical exchange, Q&A, and workshops will occur only under NDA.
3. Scope of Work
The successful vendor (the “Contractor”) shall:
Deliver a complete, integrated subsystem comprising the vendor-proposed camera array, positioning hardware, synchronized illumination, capture-control electronics, and embedded control software, meeting the performance requirements of Sections 4–7.
Provide all calibration artifacts, fixtures, and procedures required for geometric, photometric, and color calibration, including across the full Fitzpatrick I–VI tonal range.
Furnish a documented control API (REST or gRPC, vendor proposal) that exposes pose commands, capture triggers, status, fault state, and image retrieval to Aurora’s host application.
Provide a coverage-validation report demonstrating that the proposed camera count and pose schedule achieve ≥ 98% body-surface coverage on a standard anthropomorphic phantom (5th–95th percentile adult).
Deliver firmware and embedded-software architecture documentation, including the embedded control software, firmware update mechanism, and software/firmware configuration management approach.
Document the data management and local storage design, including acquisition data flow, local retention, encryption and protection of locally stored data, and hand-off to Aurora’s host environment (Section 5).
Implement and document the encryption and security requirements of Section 5.3.
Deliver design documentation, including system architecture, optical layout, positioning-system kinematics, electrical schematics, BOM, and a regulatory pre-submission gap analysis.
Provide documentation sufficient to support future transfer of the design to contract manufacturing partners, including design-for-manufacture (DFM) considerations and long-term manufacturability observations (Section 7.2).
Support Aurora’s integration testing, factory acceptance test (FAT), and site acceptance test (SAT).
Provide a 12-month warranty from final acceptance and appropriate field/service support for the POC build (Section 7.3).

5.1 Control Software, Firmware, and Embedded Systems
Embedded vendor controller exposing the API specified in SYN-6.
Documented firmware and embedded-software architecture, including the firmware update mechanism, version/configuration management, and rollback strategy (see also Section 7.1 regarding update implications for fielded units).

Raw and processed image output to a host-mounted directory or object store, with a manifest file per acquisition.
Documented data management and local storage design: acquisition data flow, buffering, local retention policy, and hand-off to Aurora’s host environment.
DICOM Visible Light Photography IOD wrapping at the system boundary (Target).
Per-frame metadata schema published as the API contract (SYN-6).
5.3 Cybersecurity, Encryption, and Data Protection
All control and data interfaces over authenticated, TLS-protected channels (encryption in transit).
Local data encryption at rest: all locally stored acquisition data, logs containing acquisition data, and buffered images shall be encrypted at rest using a current industry-standard algorithm (e.g., AES-256), with documented key management.
Protection of locally stored acquisition data against unauthorized access, including access control on service and diagnostic interfaces; the vendor shall describe its approach to data sanitization on component replacement or decommissioning.
Compliance-readiness for FDA premarket cybersecurity guidance (2023) and IEC 81001-5-1.
Documented Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) for the delivered subsystem.

7. Serviceability, Lifecycle, and Support
7.1 Lifecycle and Obsolescence Management
The vendor shall address lifecycle planning for the proposed design, including:
Component lifecycle and end-of-life (EOL) management for key components (sensors, optics, controllers, actuators), including expected availability horizons.
Mitigation planning for critical downstream obsolescence risks, including identification of single-source components and proposed alternates.
Field-component interchangeability, such that replacement components can be installed in fielded units without full-system recalibration where practical (and with a defined recalibration procedure where not).
Software/firmware update implications of component substitution, including how updates are qualified, distributed, and rolled back across fielded units.
7.2 Field Serviceability and Manufacturing Transferability
Modular replacement strategy: the design shall favor field-replaceable modules with documented replacement procedures and expected mean-time-to-repair.
Self-diagnostic capability sufficient to localize faults to the replaceable-module level.
Remote troubleshooting capability (secure remote diagnostics access, exportable logs), consistent with Section 5.3.
Overall field-service efficiency: the vendor shall describe its field-service model and the design features supporting service economics at clinical-deployment scale.
Manufacturing transferability: design documentation shall be sufficient to support future transfer of the design to contract manufacturing partners, including DFM observations, assembly and test documentation expectations, and long-term manufacturability considerations.
7.3 Warranty and POC Support
The vendor shall provide a 12-month warranty from final acceptance covering parts, labor, and software defects, together with appropriate field/service support for the POC build (response expectations to be mutually agreed at contracting). Detailed service-level requirements (SLRs), deployment geographies, and response-time expectations for production units will be defined in Phase 2; production service-tier pricing requested in Section 11 may therefore be provided on a rough-order-of-magnitude (ROM) basis with stated assumptions.
8. Regulatory and Compliance
Aurora intends to pursue FDA 510(k) clearance for the integrated DermaPod© system, and may additionally pursue Health Canada medical-device licensing; the subsystem design and documentation shall not preclude either pathway. This subsystem is a major contributor to the FDA submission, and the Contractor shall design and document the subsystem to support that path.
Consistent with the phased approach in Section 2.1, the expectations below reflect prototype-stage development. Full compliance-ready certification and production-stage quality-system expectations will be defined in Phase 2; vendors may quote certification activities as options.
IEC 60601-1 (medical electrical equipment, general safety) — design compliance required; certification optional but quotable.
IEC 60601-1-2 (EMC) — compliance required.
IEC 62471 (photobiological safety) — compliance required at the patient position, including spectral channels (ILL-6).
IEC 62304 (medical device software lifecycle) — vendor software classified per its risk class; documentation deliverables in Section 10.
ISO 14971 (risk management) — a subsystem-level risk management file is required (Section 10); Aurora maintains the system-level file.
IEC 62366-1 (usability engineering) — applied primarily at the integrated-system level by Aurora; the vendor shall support with subsystem use-related risk inputs (operator and service interactions).
ISO 10218-1/-2, ISO/TS 15066 (industrial and collaborative robots) — applicable to the powered-motion subsystem.
ISO 13485 — vendor design and manufacturing operations preferred to be ISO 13485-certified or operating under an equivalent QMS for this prototype phase; Phase 2 expectations will be defined at contracting.
21 CFR Part 820 (FDA QSR) — vendor processes shall not preclude Aurora’s 510(k) submission.
Cybersecurity per FDA premarket guidance (2023) and IEC 81001-5-1 (Section 5.3).
